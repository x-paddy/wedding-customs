Dominican Republic wedding traditions are vibrant cultural celebrations that blend Spanish colonial heritage, African influences, and indigenous Taíno customs into ceremonies lasting 2-3 days and involving 150-300 guests. Pre-Wedding Traditions and Ceremonies

What is La Pedida de Manolah peh-DEE-dah deh MAH-noh?

La pedida de mano is the formal Dominican tradition where the groom, accompanied by his parents, visits the bride’s family home to officially request permission for marriage, typically occurring 12-18 months before the wedding. This centuries-old practice, dating back to Spanish colonial times, involves a special dinner costing $500-$1,500 USD (₱28,500-₱85,500 DOP) where both families gather to discuss wedding plans and formally unite the families.

Key Components of La Pedida de Mano:

  • Formal dinner with 15-25 family members
  • Traditional gift exchange between families
  • Discussion of wedding date and arrangements
  • Blessing ceremony from family elders
  • Toast with mamajuanamah-mah-HWAH-nahmah-mah-HWAH-nah or rum

Modern Adaptations: While 78% of Dominican couples still honor this tradition, urban couples often simplify it to a restaurant dinner, while rural families maintain the full home ceremony with extended family participation.

Bendicionesben-dee-see-OH-nes: The Elder Blessing Tradition

Bendiciones (ben-dee-see-OH-nes) is the Dominican practice of seeking blessings from family elders before major life events, considered essential for marriage success. This tradition involves the couple visiting each elder family member individually, saying “Bendición, [name]” and receiving the response “Que Dios te bendiga” (May God bless you), typically completed 1-2 weeks before the wedding.

Regional Variations in Bendiciones:

  • Santiago region: Includes gift-giving to elders (usually $50-$100 USD per elder)
  • Santo Domingo: Often combined with formal family dinners
  • Rural areas: May include spiritual cleansing rituals
  • Coastal regions: Sometimes incorporates Catholic prayer sessions

Pre-Wedding Gift Delivery System

This practical custom allows couples to focus on their celebration without worrying about gift transportation and security.

Matrimonio civil is the mandatory government-officiated ceremony required by Dominican law for legal marriage recognition, costing $200-$500 USD (₱11,400-₱28,500 DOP) and typically involving 10-20 close family members. This 15-30 minute ceremony must be performed by a licensed notary public or judge at least 24 hours before any religious ceremony, with foreign couples requiring additional documentation fees of $300-$800 USD.

Civil Ceremony Requirements:

Types of Legal Marriage in Dominican Republic:

Wedding Party Traditions

Los Padrinoslohs pah-DREE-nohs: The Godparent System

Los padrinos (lohs pah-DREE-nohs) are the married couple serving as spiritual guides and official witnesses in Dominican weddings, replacing the Western tradition of multiple bridesmaids and groomsmen. Selected from respected family members or close friends, padrinos contribute $1,000-$5,000 USD toward wedding expenses and provide ongoing marriage guidance.

Padrino Responsibilities:

Las Arras: The 13 Unity Coins Ceremony

Las arraslahs AH-rahs (lahs AH-rrahs) is the sacred Dominican tradition where the groom presents 13 gold or silver coins to the bride, symbolizing Jesus and the 12 apostles and representing his commitment to provide for the family. This 5-minute ceremony segment, occurring during the religious service, uses coins costing $100-$500 USD (₱5,700-₱28,500 DOP) that often become family heirlooms passed through generations.

Las Arras Ceremony Process:

  1. Coin bearer (typically nephew aged 6-10) carries coins on silver tray
  2. Priest blesses the 13 coins with holy water
  3. Groom receives coins and counts them aloud
  4. Bride accepts coins, symbolizing shared financial responsibility
  5. Coins placed in ornate box or pouch for keeping

Regional Variations of Las Arras:

  • Northern regions: Use actual gold coins worth $1,000+ USD
  • Santo Domingo: Often use commemorative wedding coins
  • Rural areas: May include additional coins from padrinos
  • Coastal regions: Sometimes incorporate seashells with coins

La Cortège: The Wedding Procession

La cortègelah kor-TEHZH (lah cor-TEHZH) is the formal Dominican wedding procession emphasizing family unity, where the groom escorts his mother first, followed by specific pairings that symbolize the joining of two families. This 5-10 minute processional differs from Western traditions by avoiding bride/groom side seating divisions.

Traditional Procession Order:

  1. Groom with his mother (enters first)
  2. Father of groom with mother of bride
  3. Child attendants in pairs
  4. Padrinos together
  5. Bride with her father (enters last)

Church Seating Arrangement: Unlike Western “sides,” Dominican weddings feature mixed seating where both families integrate, symbolizing immediate unity. Front rows reserved for immediate family (20-30 people), with remaining guests seated by arrival order.

What documents do foreigners need to get married in Dominican Republic?

Foreign couples require extensive documentation costing $600-$1,500 USD in fees and translations, including apostilled birth certificates, single status affidavits, valid passports, and divorce decrees if applicable. Dominican wedding celebrations traditionally last 8-12 hours, beginning at sunset (6-7 PM) and continuing until sunrise (5-6 AM), with some extending into a second day. What is the difference between civil and religious ceremonies in Dominican Republic?

Civil ceremonies are legally required and cost $200-$500 USD, lasting 15-30 minutes with a government official, while religious ceremonies are optional spiritual celebrations costing $1,000-$5,000 USD and lasting 60-90 minutes. The civil ceremony must occur at least 24 hours before any religious ceremony and requires two Dominican witnesses.
