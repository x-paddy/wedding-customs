Pre-Wedding Traditions: Anuncio de Compromiso and Juramento de Affidavit

Engagement Announcement (Anuncio de Compromisoah-NOON-thee-oh deh kom-proh-MEE-soh)

Anuncio de compromisokom-proh-MEE-soh is a semi-formal engagement gathering that announces the couple’s marriage intentions, typically occurring 6-12 months before the wedding and involving 20-50 family members and close friends at a cost of GIP 500-1,500 (USD 650-1,950). This 2-4 hour evening event takes place in family homes or restaurants, where couples exchange rings and parents deliver speeches blending British formality with Spanish passionate declarations. The tradition serves as essential community bonding in Gibraltar’s close-knit society, where 79% identify as Gibraltarian.

Modern adaptations since 2020 include 70% of couples live-streaming announcements on social media for diaspora family members. Jewish families (2.4% of population) incorporate ketubahkeh-too-BAH discussions during these gatherings, while Muslim families (3.6%) focus on obtaining formal family consent. The event traditionally includes champagne toasts, family photographs, and light meals featuring local tapasTAH-pahs and olives.

Affidavit Swearing (Juramento de Affidavithoo-rah-MEN-toh deh ah-fee-DAH-veet)

Juramentohoo-rah-MEN-toh de affidavit is a mandatory legal pre-wedding procedure where couples affirm their freedom to marry before a commissioner or notary, occurring 1-2 days before the ceremony and costing GIP 67.50 (USD 88) for the special license. This 30-minute to 1-hour process requires the couple and two witnesses to present passports, birth certificates, and completed affidavit forms at the commissioner’s office. The tradition highlights Gibraltar’s efficient marriage laws that attract thousands of international couples annually.

This requirement remained unchanged during COVID-19, with masks and social distancing protocols added. Non-residents must complete this step immediately upon arrival, reinforcing Gibraltar’s overnight stay rule for marriages. Local couples often combine the affidavit swearing with family lunches, transforming a legal necessity into a social occasion. Post-2022 adaptations include online pre-submissions for increased efficiency.

Wedding Day Ceremonies: Civil Registration, Religious Blessings, and Arras

Civil Ceremony (Ceremonia Civiltheh-reh-MOH-nee-ah thee-VEEL)

Ceremonia civil is the mandatory legal marriage ceremony conducted in English at approved Gibraltar venues, lasting 15-30 minutes and involving 4-20 participants including the couple, two required witnesses, and immediate family. This core tradition costs GIP 100-500 (USD 130-650) and takes place at the Registry Office, Botanical Gardens, or the ornate Mayor’s Parlour with its terrace views of the Mediterranean. The ceremony follows British legal procedures with vow exchanges, ring presentations, and marriage certificate signing.

During 2020-2024, 80% of ceremonies incorporated live-streaming for remote family members, particularly benefiting the 13.2% British resident population with UK connections. Hindu couples (2% of population) occasionally add symbolic fire rituals when venue permits allow, while all ceremonies maintain the legal requirement of two witnesses over age 18. The straightforward nature appeals to international couples seeking efficient, legally-recognized marriages.

Religious Blessing (Bendición Religiosaben-dee-THEE-ohn reh-lee-HEE-oh-sah)

Bendiciónben-dee-THEE-ohn religiosa is an optional religious ceremony following the civil marriage, typically lasting 1-2 hours and involving 50-150 participants at churches, synagogues, mosques, or temples with fees of GIP 200-1,000 (USD 260-1,300). This tradition reflects Gibraltar’s religious diversity, with 72.9% Catholic population favoring traditional masses including hymns and communion, while Jewish ceremonies (2.4%) feature the chuppahkhoo-PAH canopy and Muslim nikahnee-KAH3.6% involve marriage contracts. The dual civil-religious system demonstrates Gibraltar’s historical tolerance and multicultural heritage.

Each religious tradition requires specific elements: holy texts, ordained clergy, and symbolic items such as veils for Catholic brides or prayer shawls for Jewish grooms.

The Coin Ceremony (ArrasAH-rahs)

Arras is a Spanish-influenced wedding tradition where the groom presents 13 gold or silver coins to the bride, symbolizing his commitment to provide for the family and occurring during the ceremony after vows for 5-10 minutes. This ritual costs GIP 50-200 (USD 65-260) for custom coins and involves only the couple, though family witnesses add emotional significance. The 13 coins represent Jesus and the 12 apostles, blessed by the priest or officiant before being poured between the couple’s hands three times to symbolize shared prosperity and trust.

The tradition gained renewed popularity during 2020-2024 hybrid ceremonies for diaspora couples, with 60% choosing engraved coins featuring wedding dates. Spanish-influenced families (2.1% of population) typically incorporate more elaborate versions, while British families often simplify the ritual. Moroccan groups may include similar dowry symbols reflecting their cultural heritage. The coins become family heirlooms, emphasizing enduring commitment in Gibraltar’s multicultural society where Catholic, British, and Mediterranean influences blend seamlessly.
