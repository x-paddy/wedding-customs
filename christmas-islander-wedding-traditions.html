Pre-Wedding Traditions: Khastgari, Tea Ceremony, and Henna Night

Tea Ceremony (Cha Daochah-dow)

Cha Dao is a Chinese betrothal tradition that honors ancestors through serving tea to elders, typically occurring 1-2 days before the main wedding and involving 20-50 family members at a cost of AUD 1,000-3,000 (USD 650-1,950).

The ceremony takes place in family homes or community halls, where the bride and groom kneel before parents and elders, serving tea infused with local wild limes. In return, they receive ang powahng-pow (red envelopes) containing money and marriage advice. Red lanterns decorate phosphate-rock gardens during the ritual, while incense burns at home altars. This tradition reinforces Chinese cultural identity within Christmas Island’s diaspora community, with 60% of Chinese-descent couples maintaining this practice despite the island’s isolation.

Modern adaptations: Since 2020, 50% of couples use digital red envelopes via apps, and ceremonies incorporate eco-friendly local herbs. Virtual attendance through Zoom allows overseas relatives to participate, addressing travel limitations to this remote territory.

Henna Night (Mehendimeh-HEN-dee Application)

Mehendi is a Malay pre-wedding beautification ritual that adorns the bride’s hands and feet with intricate henna designs, occurring 2-3 days before the wedding with 30-60 female participants at a cost of AUD 800-2,000 (USD 520-1,300).

Held at the bride’s home, this 2-hour celebration features imported henna paste applied in patterns incorporating local motifs like Christmas Island red crabs. Women gather to dance, sing traditional songs, and feast on Malay sweets while the bride’s hands are decorated. The ritual symbolizes joy and prosperity for the upcoming marriage, with darker henna stains believed to indicate deeper love from the groom.

Regional variations: Indian-influenced Malay families incorporate more elaborate peacock and mandala designs, while traditional Malay patterns focus on floral motifs. Post-COVID adaptations include DIY henna kits for smaller gatherings and professional artists conducting sessions via video guidance.

Hair-Combing Ceremony (Shang Toushahng-toh)

Shang Tou is a Chinese purification ritual where parents comb the couple’s hair for good fortune, taking place the night before the wedding with 10-20 close family members at a cost of AUD 300-800 (USD 195-520).

This 30-minute ceremony occurs in separate family homes, where parents use special combs while reciting blessings for fertility, longevity, and harmony. Each stroke represents a wish: first for lifelong union, second for harmony, third for many children, fourth for good fortune. The ritual concludes with the couple looking in mirrors to see their transformed selves ready for marriage.

Modern practice: Gender-neutral adaptations now include same-sex couples, reflecting Australia’s 2017 marriage equality laws. Traditionally followed by the tea ceremony, this creates a two-part pre-wedding sequence honoring family bonds.

Wedding Day Ceremonies: Nikah, Church Vows, and Bersanding

Bersanding is a Malay royal presentation tradition where the married couple sits on an ornate throne to receive community blessings, occurring the afternoon of the wedding with 80-120 participants at a cost of AUD 3,000-6,000 (USD 1,950-3,900).

Reception Traditions: Lion Dance, Unity Bowl, and Communal Feast

Lion Dance (Chinese Prosperity Ritual)

Lion dance is a Chinese reception opener that wards off evil spirits and brings prosperity to newlyweds, performed at the start of evening feasts with 100-150 guests at a cost of AUD 2,500-5,000 (USD 1,625-3,250).

The 30-minute performance features two dancers in elaborate lion costumes performing acrobatic movements to drum beats. Christmas Island adaptations use local drummers from the Chinese Association, incorporating island rhythms into traditional patterns. The lion “eats” lettuce hung high with red envelopes, then “spits” it toward the couple for good fortune. Firecrackers, where permitted, add excitement.

Required elements: Professional lion costume (often shipped from Perth), experienced dancers, lettuce with hong baohong-bow, drum ensemble. Southern Chinese styles emphasize more acrobatic movements compared to northern variations.

Modern practice: Post-2020, smaller troupes perform due to travel restrictions. 40% of receptions now feature recorded music when live drummers unavailable, though live performance remains strongly preferred for authenticity.

Unity Bowl (Australian Blending Ritual)

Unity bowl is a contemporary Australian ceremony element where couples combine materials symbolizing their union, integrated into vows with 50-100 witnesses at a cost of AUD 500-1,000 (USD 325-650).

During the 10-minute ritual, bride and groom simultaneously pour different colored sands or shells into a decorative bowl, creating unique patterns representing their individual lives merging. Christmas Island couples often use red volcanic soil from the island mixed with white beach sand, symbolizing the island’s unique geology and their roots in this remote territory.

This tradition enables interfaith couples to include a unifying element without religious connotations. Traditionally followed by ring exchange, it provides a visual representation of marriage that guests can witness. The completed bowl becomes a home keepsake.

Modern adaptations: Virtual pouring ceremonies allow distant relatives to participate by mailing sand from their locations. Environmental consciousness drives 80% of couples to use locally sourced materials rather than imported colored sand.

Post-Wedding Communal Barbecue

Communal barbecue is a relaxed feast tradition held the day after formal ceremonies, bringing together 80-120 community members at beaches or parks at a cost of AUD 4,000-8,000 (USD 2,600-5,200).

The 3-4 hour gathering features grilled seafood including the island’s famous red crabs (when in season), prawns, and fish alongside salads and tropical fruits. This informal setting allows different cultural groups to mingle freely, with halal and vegetarian options ensuring inclusive participation. Toasts continue from various community members, sharing stories and blessings for the couple.

This tradition reflects Australian barbecue culture adapted to Christmas Island’s multicultural context. Unlike formal receptions, dress codes relax and children play freely while adults share local phosphate beer and imported wines. Live music often features mixed cultural performances.

Regional variations: Malay families ensure completely halal preparation with separate grills. Chinese families might include whole roasted fish for abundance symbolism. During COVID, takeaway boxes replaced communal serving, maintaining safety while preserving tradition.
